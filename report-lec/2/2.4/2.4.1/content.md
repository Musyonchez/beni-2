# 2.4.1 University of Nairobi (UoN) Wayfinding Maps, Kenya

The University of Nairobi (UoN) has developed online and printable wayfinding maps that display the layout of its main and satellite campuses. These maps provide detailed information on the locations of lecture halls, faculty buildings, administrative offices, and student service areas. While the system is primarily static and lacks real-time GPS tracking, it plays a crucial role in helping users navigate the university's vast environment. UoN's approach highlights the local need for more interactive and digital navigation systems that can provide real-time guidance for both students and visitors.

Figure 8 and 9 above show The University of Nairobi's Wayfinding Maps system demonstrates an important local effort toward structured campus navigation and spatial organization. By offering both online and printable map formats, the university ensures accessibility for a wide range of users, including those with limited internet access or older mobile devices. The maps are well-labeled and organized according to faculties, lecture halls, and key administrative areas, making them useful for first-time visitors, new students, and staff. The inclusion of both main and satellite campuses also reflects the university's awareness of its broad geographical footprint and the need for unified navigation support across locations.

Despite being static and lacking dynamic GPS or interactive routing features, the system effectively fulfills its core objective: to provide clear visual references that help users locate essential facilities within the institution. Its simple, graphic-oriented design promotes quick interpretation, which is particularly beneficial in a setting with high pedestrian traffic and multiple entry points. Moreover, the use of standardized icons and color codes across the maps enhances consistency and reduces the likelihood of confusion for users unfamiliar with the campus layout.

From a local perspective, UoN's initiative highlights both the progress and limitations of digital mapping within Kenyan universities. It underscores the ongoing transition from static representations to more interactive, data-driven solutions. For the USIU Campus Navigation System, the UoN model offers valuable lessons in prioritizing clarity, accessibility, and comprehensiveness before implementing advanced features. Building upon this foundation, USIU can incorporate interactive elements such as real-time positioning, voice-assisted directions, and building search filters to create a more advanced and engaging platform. Ultimately, this evolution from static maps to smart navigation technology represents the next step in improving campus mobility and user experience within Kenyan higher education institutions.
