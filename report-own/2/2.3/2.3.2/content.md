# 2.3.2 Campus Gusto (Nigeria)

Campus Gusto is a subscription-based mobile meal application designed specifically for university students in Nigeria. Rather than operating a single institutional cafeteria, the platform connects students to multiple vendors located near their campus. Students subscribe to their preferred vendors, browse available menus, and schedule daily meal deliveries to their location, eliminating the need to physically queue at a canteen. Orders are tracked through a dedicated Orders tab, where each entry displays the vendor name, scheduled date and time, and current order status. An Account section provides access to personal information, login and security settings, and app support resources.

Figure 8 shows the Account screen, which consolidates personal information management, login and security settings, privacy policy, and support links into a single profile area, alongside Logout and Delete Account options.

Campus Gusto addresses one of the core problems common to African university food service: long physical queues at peak hours. By moving order placement off the counter and into a mobile scheduling interface, the app allows kitchen preparation to begin before the student arrives, and delivery scheduling removes the student from the queue entirely. The multi-vendor model also reflects the practical reality of many African campuses, where food service is distributed across multiple informal or semi-formal vendors rather than a single institutional cafeteria.

The platform's limitations in the context of this project are primarily structural. Campus Gusto is a commercial third-party service rather than an institutionally managed system, meaning it does not integrate with a university wallet or student account. Payment and vendor relationships are managed externally. There is no in-app wallet, no staff-facing order management interface, and no push notification for order readiness visible in the reviewed version. For the USIU Cafeteria Ordering System, Campus Gusto validates the demand for mobile meal scheduling in the Nigerian — and by extension African — university context, while the absence of an institutional wallet and real-time staff coordination underscores the features this project adds.
