# 2.6 Conclusion

The literature reviewed in this chapter demonstrates that while food ordering technology has advanced considerably at the global level, its application within university cafeterias — particularly in the African and Kenyan context — remains severely limited. Global platforms such as MIT Dining and NUS uNivUS prove that mobile ordering, digital wallets, and real-time tracking are technically achievable and operationally effective in university dining environments. Regional and local systems, however, reveal that these capabilities have not yet reached Kenyan institutions, with the most advanced local example being a static QR code menu that offers no ordering functionality.

This gap is both a challenge and an opportunity. It confirms that the problem identified in Chapter One — physical queuing, no menu visibility, no pre-ordering, cash-only transactions, and no staff demand visibility — is not unique to USIU-A, but is in fact the norm across local universities. It also confirms that no existing local system has attempted to address this problem comprehensively through a mobile-first platform.

The proposed USIU Cafeteria Ordering System is designed to close this gap by delivering capabilities that exceed those of every local and regional system reviewed: native Android ordering, real-time status tracking with estimated wait time, advance meal scheduling with recurring options, automated wallet deduction at cut-off, and role-based access for both students and staff. The next chapter formalises the aims and objectives that guide this system's development.
