## Analysis

The systems reviewed span a wide spectrum from fully manual operations to sophisticated mobile platforms. Global systems such as the MIT Dining App and NUS uNivUS demonstrate what is possible when institutional resources and infrastructure are aligned behind a digital food service strategy. Both deliver genuine improvements to student experience through menu visibility, digital payment, and order tracking. However, neither offers advance meal scheduling or recurring order functionality, and both are too deeply embedded in their respective institutional ecosystems to serve as direct models for a student project at a mid-sized university.

Regional systems, particularly UCT Hungry Rhino, validate the core concept of this project — pre-ordering with collection slots demonstrably reduces peak-hour congestion in an African university cafeteria. This is the most directly relevant precedent in the literature. However, its web-only delivery, lack of push notifications, and absence of a persistent wallet leave clear room for improvement in a native mobile implementation.

Local systems reveal the extent of the gap in the Kenyan university context. UoN represents the status quo: entirely manual, with no digital engagement whatsoever. Strathmore represents the current frontier of local innovation: a QR code menu that proves student willingness to use their phone for food-related information, but offers no ordering capability. No Kenyan university cafeteria currently offers mobile ordering, digital payment through an app, real-time order tracking, pre-scheduling, or an in-app wallet.

The proposed USIU Cafeteria Ordering System addresses all six dimensions identified in the gap analysis — pre-ordering, recurring scheduling, real-time tracking, native mobile delivery, in-app wallet, and local university context — making it the first system of its kind in the Kenyan university landscape.
