# 1.2 Problem Statement

The USIU-Africa cafeteria serves a large and growing student population across multiple meal periods each day. Despite its central role in campus life, the cafeteria operates without any digital ordering infrastructure, creating a set of interconnected problems that affect both students and cafeteria staff on a daily basis.

During peak hours — particularly between 10:00 AM and 11:00 AM, and again between 1:00 PM and 2:00 PM — queues of twenty to forty students are not uncommon. These windows coincide directly with breaks between lectures, meaning students have a limited and fixed amount of time to obtain a meal and return to class. A queue of this size can easily exceed the available break time, forcing students to choose between eating and attending their next class on time. The result is missed meals, wasted time, and unnecessary stress that undermines the academic experience.

Compounding the queue problem is the complete absence of menu visibility. Students have no way to check what is available before walking to the cafeteria, which means those who arrive to find their preferred meal sold out must either settle for an alternative or leave without eating. There is no mechanism to browse the menu, check item availability, or make an informed choice before committing the time to travel to the counter.

Pre-ordering is equally absent. Food preparation does not begin until a student reaches the counter and places a verbal order, meaning preparation time is added on top of queue time. Students with fixed lecture timetables have no way to schedule a meal in advance and ensure it is ready at a specific time. A student whose lecture ends at 12:15 cannot arrange to have food waiting at 12:30 — they must queue and wait like everyone else regardless of how predictable their schedule is.

From the staff perspective, the absence of a digital system means there is no advance visibility into demand. Cafeteria staff cannot know how many portions of a given item will be needed in the next hour, leading to both stock shortages during peak demand and food wastage when demand is overestimated. Without data on order volumes and timing patterns, operational planning remains reactive rather than proactive.

Finally, every transaction requires cash. Students must carry money to every meal, and staff must manage a cash float throughout the day. This adds friction for students and administrative overhead for staff, and stands in contrast to the cashless payment norms that have become standard in Kenyan commercial food service.

There is a clear and pressing need for a dedicated mobile ordering system for the USIU-A cafeteria — one that eliminates physical queuing, provides menu visibility, enables advance meal scheduling, and supports cashless payment through an in-app wallet.
