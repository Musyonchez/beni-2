# 1.1 History

The evolution of food ordering systems closely mirrors the broader trajectory of digital technology and mobile computing. In the early decades of institutional food service, ordering was an entirely manual process: students and staff would join a physical queue, select from whatever was displayed or described at the counter, and pay in cash. While straightforward, this model placed the entire burden of demand management on the moment of service, with no mechanism for advance planning on either the customer or provider side.

The introduction of digital point-of-sale (POS) terminals in the 1990s marked the first significant shift, replacing handwritten tickets and cash drawers with electronic transaction records. Fast food chains such as McDonald's and KFC were early adopters, using POS systems to accelerate throughput and capture sales data. However, POS terminals addressed speed at the counter — they did not eliminate the queue or provide customers with any form of advance ordering.

The 2000s brought online ordering to the mainstream. Platforms such as Domino's and Pizza Hut launched web-based ordering systems that allowed customers to place orders from home before arriving for collection or delivery. This was the first widespread separation of the ordering moment from the service moment, demonstrating that demand could be captured and prepared in advance. The concept was extended in the 2010s with the rise of dedicated mobile applications — McDonald's, Starbucks, and a wave of food delivery platforms including Uber Eats and Jumia Food introduced app-based ordering with real-time status tracking and, in some cases, integrated digital payment.

In the Kenyan context, mobile money through M-Pesa fundamentally transformed the payment landscape, normalising cashless transactions even at informal food vendors. Food delivery services such as Jumia Food and Glovo Kenya demonstrated that mobile ordering was viable and in demand in the local market. Nonetheless, the institutional cafeteria setting — particularly within universities — has largely been left behind. Most Kenyan university cafeterias continue to operate on fully manual processes: physical queuing, cash-only transactions, and no digital menu visibility.

This history reveals a consistent pattern: the technology to solve institutional cafeteria queuing has existed for over a decade, but its application in the Kenyan university context remains almost entirely absent. The gap between what is technically possible and what is operationally deployed at institutions like USIU-A is the motivation for this project.
